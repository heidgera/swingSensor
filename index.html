Compressed air energy storage is a promising technology which may allow us to store energy gathered from renewable sources for later use.

However, it's not a perfect storage solution; compressing air generates heat which might be lost, and expanding compressed air to reclaim energy requires heat to reach max efficiency.

If the heat from compression can be stored for use during expansion, the efficiency of the system can increase dramatically.

Use the air compressors to either side to explore one way that researchers are trying to store heat for later use.
Compress air in the Right Compressor…

and hold for 1, 2, 3. Watch the Graph.

Notice this:

The Right Graph doesn’t rise as high as the Left one did but it doesn’t fall as far either. Not as much heat and pressure is lost.

What’s happening?

Find the heat sink—a rolled up screen in the right compressor. The screen absorbs some of the heat you generate, which is then stored in the water. Since air in the right compressor didn’t heat up as much, you didn’t work as hard to compress it.
Expand the air in the Right Cylinder.
Compare the Right Graph to the Left Graph.

Notice this:
The area between the two curves curves—the record of your work—on the Right Graph is smaller than this area on the Left Graph.

What’s happening?
The heat-trapping screen nested in the Right Compressor recycles the heat you generate, resulting in less wasted work. So what? This experiment shows promise for heat-recycling research underway at the University of Minnesota’s Center for Compact and Efficient Fluid Power.

Compress air in the Left Compressor…

and hold for 1, 2, 3. Watch the Graph and the Pressure Gauge respond.

Notice this:

The graph and the pressure gauge show the air pressure rising high at first, but then dropping a little.

What’s happening?

When you compress air, you generate heat and pressure. As some of that heat escapes through the Compressor walls into the surrounding air, the pressure drops a bit.
Expand air in the Left Cylinder.
Watch the Left Graph respond.

Notice this:
The pressure and temperature drops. The graph returns to zero, but the expansion line—or bottom curve—lies considerably below the compression line—or top curve.

So what?
The top curve of the graph represents your work compressing the air. The bottom curve represents work done by the air as it expands. The area between the curves shows how much work you have lost in the compression – expansion cycle through heat loss.

It takes work to compress air and generate heat. Lost heat is wasted work.
